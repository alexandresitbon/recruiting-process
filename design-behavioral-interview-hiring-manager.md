description: Objective, questions, and rubric for a 30min product design behavioral interview done by a hiring manager
---

## Interview objective

This interview is designed to assess the character and values behind the candidate's work — their drive, curiosity, honesty, and willingness to act with agency and urgency without waiting to be told. The goal is to understand who this person is and if they align with the operating values of the company.

## Interview questions 

- [Drive] Tell me about something you built or created — inside or outside of work — that nobody asked you to do.
- [Curiosity] What's something about design — or an adjacent field — that you've gone deep on recently, just because you wanted to?
- [Ethics] Tell us about a time you were wrong about something at work. How did you find out, and what did you do?
- [Agency] Walk me through a time you made a significant decision without having all the information or buy-in you would have wanted.
- [Urgency] Tell me about a time you had to ship something faster than you were comfortable with. How did you handle it?
- [Ownership] Tell me about something that went wrong on a project you were part of. What was your role in it?
- [AI] Walk me through a workflow you've built using AI that other people on your team now rely on. What problem did you solve, and how did you build it?

## Interview rubric

Score each area 1–4. Use the descriptors to anchor your score. Fill out immediately after the interview.
## 1. Drive

_Question: Tell me about something you built or created — inside or outside of work — that nobody asked you to do._

| Score | Descriptor                                                                                                                                                                             |
| ----- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1     | Can't point to anything self-initiated. Answer defaults to work they were assigned or expected to do.                                                                                  |
| 2     | Has an example but it's thin — a minor side project or something loosely self-directed. Hard to tell if it reflects genuine drive or just a slow week.                                 |
| 3     | Clear example of something they initiated and saw through on their own. The motivation feels real and the output was meaningful.                                                       |
| 4     | Compelling, specific example of something built entirely on their own initiative and a clear sense of why they couldn't not do it. The energy when they talk about it is unmistakable. |

## 2. Curiosity

_Question: What's something about design — or an adjacent field — that you've gone deep on recently, just because you wanted to?_

| Score | Descriptor                                                                                                                                                                                                 |
| ----- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1     | No evidence of genuine intellectual curiosity. Mentions a tool or trend they've heard of but can't go deep.                                                                                                |
| 2     | Has something they've looked into but stays surface level.                                                                                                                                                 |
| 3     | Clear area of genuine interest they've gone deep on. Can speak specifically about what they've learned.                                                                                                    |
| 4     | Lights up talking about it. The depth is real — they've read, experimented, formed opinions, and can connect it back to their work in a non-obvious way. Makes me feel like I learned something from them. |

## 3. Ethics / Honesty

_Question: Tell us about a time you were wrong about something at work. How did you find out, and what did you do?_

| Score | Descriptor                                                                                                                                                          |
| ----- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1     | Struggles to identify a real example. Answer is defensive, vague, or reframes the mistake as someone else's fault.                                                  |
| 2     | Has an example but minimizes it. The "wrong" thing is small or softened. Doesn't fully own their role in it.                                                        |
| 3     | Honest, specific example. Clearly owns what they got wrong, explains how they found out, and describes what they did to address it.                                 |
| 4     | Remarkably candid. The example is real and significant, the self-awareness is genuine, and the way they talk about it shows they've actually integrated the lesson. |

## 4. Agency

_Question: Walk me through a time you made a significant decision without having all the information or buy-in you would have wanted._

| Score | Descriptor                                                                                                                                                                                   |
| ----- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1     | Can't point to a real example. Defaults to decisions that were either minor or fully sanctioned. Uncomfortable with the premise of acting without full buy-in.                               |
| 2     | Has an example but the decision was low-stakes or they waited until they had near-full information before acting. Doesn't demonstrate real comfort with ambiguity.                           |
| 3     | Clear example of a meaningful decision made with incomplete information or without full alignment. Can articulate how they weighed the tradeoffs and what they learned.                      |
| 4     | Confident, specific example where the stakes were real and the ambiguity was genuine. Shows they can make a call, own it, and course-correct if needed — without needing permission to move. |

## 5. Urgency

_Question: Tell me about a time you had to ship something faster than you were comfortable with. How did you handle it?_

|Score|Descriptor|
|---|---|
|1|Resisted the pace or defaulted to slowing things down. No evidence of being able to operate under real time pressure.|
|2|Got through it but reactively. Didn't demonstrate a clear framework for how they triaged or what they sacrificed and why.|
|3|Handled it well. Made deliberate tradeoffs, communicated clearly about what was and wasn't possible, and shipped without the wheels falling off.|
|4|Thrived under it. Shows a clear mental model for how to move fast without losing quality — knows what to cut, what to protect, and how to keep the team aligned when time is short.|

## 6. Ownership

_Question: Tell me about something that went wrong on a project you were part of. What was your role in it?_

| Score | Descriptor                                                                                                                                                               |
| ----- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| 1     | Deflects or minimizes. Blames the team, the timeline, or external factors. Hard to identify what they actually owned.                                                    |
| 2     | Acknowledges the problem but their role in it is vague. Takes partial ownership without being fully accountable.                                                         |
| 3     | Clearly owns their part in what went wrong — including decisions they made or things they failed to catch. Doesn't over-explain or blame others.                         |
| 4     | Owns it fully and specifically. Can trace their decisions or inactions directly to the outcome, explain what they'd do differently, and do so without self-flagellation. |

## 7. AI Fluency

_Question: Walk me through a workflow you've built using AI that other people on your team now rely on. What problem did you solve, and how did you build it?_

| Score | Descriptor                                                                                                                                                        |
| ----- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1     | No real example. Talks about using AI personally (e.g. writing prompts, generating images) but nothing that scaled to the team or solved a systemic problem.      |
| 2     | Has something but it's basic — a shared prompt template or a simple automation. Doesn't demonstrate systems thinking or meaningful team impact.                   |
| 3     | Clear example of a workflow they built that others now use. Can explain the problem, what they built, how they built it, and the before/after impact on the team. |
| 4     | Built something genuinely impressive — a workflow, tool, or system that changed how the team operates. Shows they are AI-native.                                  |

## 8. STAR Communication

_Evaluated across all answers — how well does the candidate structure their responses?_

| Score | Descriptor                                                                                                                                                                    |
| ----- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1     | Answers are rambling, circular, or missing key elements. Hard to follow what happened, what they did, or what the outcome was.                                                |
| 2     | Some structure but inconsistent. Either spends too long on context and runs out of time for the result, or skips straight to the outcome without enough setup to evaluate it. |
| 3     | Answers are clear and well-structured. Situation is set up efficiently, their specific actions are distinguishable from the team's, and the result is concrete.               |
| 4     | Exceptionally crisp. Every answer has a clear arc — just enough context, a specific and personal account of what they did, and a result that's concrete and honest.           |

## Hiring Signal

|Total Score|Signal|
|---|---|
|27–32|Strong Yes|
|20–26|Yes|
|13–19|No|
|Below 13|Strong No|
