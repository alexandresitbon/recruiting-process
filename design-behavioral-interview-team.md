## Interview objective

This interview is designed to assess how the candidate operates in practice — how they navigate technical constraints, influence stakeholders, handle conflicting feedback, and take ownership of quality and business impact. The goal is to understand how this person works with others and whether they're ready to lead from within a cross-functional team.
## Interview questions 
- [Business impact] Tell me about a time you shipped something where you weren't sure if it worked. How did you define success, and what did you find out?
- [Quality] Tell me about a time something you designed shipped in a way that looked or behaved differently than what you'd spec'd. How did you find out, and what did you do about it?
- [AI] Tell me about a time you designed an interface where you couldn't rely on established patterns — something where the interaction model itself was unclear or didn't exist yet. How did you figure out what to build, and what did you get wrong the first time?
- [Influence & Communication] Tell me about a time you had to convince someone senior — a director, VP, or C-level exec — to change their mind and align with you design solution. How did you approach it?
- [Collaboration] Tell me about a design decision you made specifically to accommodate a technical constraint. How did you decide what to give up, and how did you feel about the outcome?
- [Feedback & Conflict] Tell me about a time you received a significant amount of conflicting design feedback from stakeholders. How did you sort through it and decide what to act on?
-  [Leadership]Tell me about a process or ritual you introduced or improved on a team you were part of. What was broken, what did you change, and what was the result?

## Interview rubric

Score each area 1–4. Use the descriptors to anchor your score. Fill out immediately after the interview.

## 1. Business Impact

_Question: Tell me about a time you shipped something where you weren't sure if it worked. How did you define success, and what did you find out?_

| Score | Descriptor                                                                                                                                                                   |
| ----- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1     | Can't articulate how they defined success.                                                                                                                                   |
| 2     | Has a vague sense of success metrics but didn't drive them. Outcome is unclear or they found out second-hand without really digging in.                                      |
| 3     | Defined success upfront, tracked it, and can speak to what they found — including if the result was mixed or inconclusive. Shows they care about outcomes, not just outputs. |
| 4     | Proactively set up the conditions to measure their work and used what they found to make a concrete next decision.                                                           |

## 2. Quality

_Question: Tell me about a time something you designed shipped in a way that looked or behaved differently than what you'd spec'd. How did you find out, and what did you do about it?_

| Score | Descriptor                                                                                                                                                                                   |
| ----- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1     | Can't recall an example, or dismisses fidelity gaps as inevitable and not worth fighting.                                                                                                    |
| 2     | Has an example but handled it passively — noticed the gap, maybe mentioned it, but didn't drive a resolution. Treats it as engineering's problem.                                            |
| 3     | Noticed the gap, raised it clearly, and worked with engineering to close it — without being precious about their original design. Can articulate what they prioritized and what they let go. |
| 4     | Has a sharp point of view on quality and actively builds habits or systems to prevent these gaps. They fixed the problem by shipping a fix in production on their own.                       |

## 3. AI Fluency

_Question: Tell me about a time you designed an interface where you couldn't rely on established patterns — something where the interaction model itself was unclear or didn't exist yet. How did you figure out what to build, and what did you get wrong the first time?_

| Score | Descriptor                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| ----- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1     | No real example. Defaults to conventional product design work where patterns were well established.                                                                                                                       |
| 2     | Has an example of ambiguous design work but it's not genuinely novel — the patterns existed, they just hadn't used them before.                                                                                           |
| 3     | Clear example of designing something where the interaction model was undefined. Can explain how they approached it, what they tried, and what they got wrong with specificity.                                            |
| 4     | Has navigated truly novel design territory and can speak to it with depth — what mental models they borrowed, what failed and why, and how the experience changed how they think about designing for emerging technology. |

## 4. Influence & Communication

_Question: Tell me about a time you had to convince someone senior — a director, VP, or C-level exec — to change their mind and align with your design solution. How did you approach it?_

| Score | Descriptor                                                                                                                                                      |
| ----- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1     | Can't point to a real example. Hasn't operated at that level.                                                                                                   |
| 2     | Has an example but their approach was passive — presented the work and hoped it landed. Didn't demonstrate influential communication.                           |
| 3     | Prepared thoughtfully, understood what the senior person cared about, and framed the design solution in terms that landed.                                      |
| 4     | Shows a mature understanding of how to move people in positions of power — reads the room, leads with what matters to them, and builds the case layer by layer. |

## 5. Collaboration

_Question: Tell me about a design decision you made specifically to accommodate a technical constraint. How did you decide what to give up, and how did you feel about the outcome?_

| Score | Descriptor                                                                                                                                                                                        |
| ----- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1     | Resents technical constraints or treats them as someone else's problem. No evidence of partnership with engineering.                                                                              |
| 2     | Accepted the constraint but passively — didn't engage with the tradeoff or advocate for the best outcome within it.                                                                               |
| 3     | Engaged with engineering to understand the constraint, made a deliberate call about what to sacrifice, and can explain why.                                                                       |
| 4     | Treats technical constraints as a creative challenge, not an obstacle. Shows genuine curiosity about how things are built and uses that understanding to find solutions that work for both sides. |

## 6. Feedback & Conflict

_Question: Tell me about a time you received a significant amount of conflicting design feedback from stakeholders. How did you sort through it and decide what to act on?_

| Score | Descriptor                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| ----- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1     | Tried to act on everything or got paralyzed by the conflict. No clear framework for how they prioritized.                                                                                                                       |
| 2     | Made a call but can't explain the reasoning clearly. Either deferred to the loudest voice or avoided the conflict rather than resolving it.                                                                                     |
| 3     | Had a clear approach to sorting through conflicting feedback — anchored on user needs, business goals, or a shared definition of success — and can explain how they handled the stakeholders whose feedback they didn't act on. |
| 4     | Navigated the conflict with sophistication. Identified the underlying disagreement beneath the surface feedback, addressed it directly, and brought stakeholders along rather than just overriding them.                        |

## 7. Leadership

_Question: Tell me about a process or ritual you introduced or improved on a team you were part of. What was broken, what did you change, and what was the result?_

| Score | Descriptor                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| ----- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| 1     | Can't point to an example. Participated in team processes but never took initiative to shape them.                                                                                                                 |
| 2     | Has an example but it's minor — a small tweak to an existing process rather than something they identified and drove.                                                                                              |
| 3     | Identified something that wasn't working, proposed a change, got buy-in, and implemented it. Can speak to the result with specificity.                                                                             |
| 4     | Diagnosed a systemic problem, designed a solution that fit the team's culture, and built something that stuck. Shows they lead from within — shaping how the team works without needing a formal mandate to do it. |

## 8. STAR Communication

_Evaluated across all answers — how well does the candidate structure their responses?_

| Score | Descriptor                                                                                                                                                                    |
| ----- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1     | Answers are rambling, circular, or missing key elements. Hard to follow what happened, what they did, or what the outcome was.                                                |
| 2     | Some structure but inconsistent. Either spends too long on context and runs out of time for the result, or skips straight to the outcome without enough setup to evaluate it. |
| 3     | Answers are clear and well-structured. Situation is set up efficiently, their specific actions are distinguishable from the team's, and the result is concrete.               |
| 4     | Exceptionally crisp. Every answer has a clear arc — just enough context, a specific and personal account of what they did, and a result that's concrete and honest.           |
